Chapter 01

The Advantage of a Strong Mental Game

It's called a blueprint for a reason. These pages help you understand why the mind slips into a protective state of avoiding adversity, the signs your body is under pressure, and a step-by-step toolkit to stay focused, sharp and high-performing in any situation.

As an ex-basketballer, it's immediately evident to me how much more is demanded of our athletes just to stay in the mix for top teams. They're doing huge amounts of extra training — yet no one is guiding these young athletes through the mental hurdles they face, or helping them build the fortitude to keep going.

Early on, using the skills I developed as a player and a performance coach, I decided I'd arm my boys with the mindset tools they needed — not just to have an edge, but to thrive and be happy while working hard toward their goals. In three short years they've been selected for State-level high-performance squads and the top teams at their competitive club, won a State Championship, and taken home a Bronze medal at the U14 Club National Championships. A huge part of those milestones was the time we invested in their mental and emotional acuity.

This guide helps parents and junior athletes build resilience, focus and consistency under pressure — to perform at their best and enjoy the journey without burnout, plus specific communication tools that limit the feeling of pressure and expectation.

Chapter 02

About Me & My Sons

One of the skills I was most proud of as a player was the ability to never be swayed from my focus. The game on the line, seconds to go, the coach yelling — that was my favourite place on earth.

Full transparency: my boys had the "advantage" of a massive life trauma when they were young. Their dad went into heart failure, leading to many life-and-death situations they witnessed. He eventually had a heart transplant, and the boys were there through the whole recovery. The tools we used to navigate that — and the skills they built to prepare their minds for the worst case — are the same ones they use now to overcome high-pressure situations on the court.

Investing time in your athlete's mental wellbeing gives them an edge rarely seen nowadays. As physical demands increase, no one is paying attention to the mental load that comes with them. This is your opportunity to get a head start — not just to gain an advantage, but more importantly, to protect your child.

Sport is the only place where those hard emotions should be welcomed regularly.

As parents, we try to minimise our children being uncomfortable or in any situation where they must navigate adversity. So often I see parents helping kids avoid pressure instead of helping them walk through it — which is detrimental to their development as an athlete. Pre-emptively building strategies for them to overcome high-pressure scenarios is what serves them best.

Chapter 03

Understanding the Pressure of a Game

Giving your kids the tools to independently manage high-pressure, in-game scenarios arms them for similar moments in life — and for any disappointments down the track in their sport.

Advantages of pressure in junior sport
  • Learning to manage anxiety
  • Developing the ability to self-assess
  • Developing quick thinking in a high-pressure moment
  • The ability to overcome things quickly and move on
  • Improving self-talk as a tool to overcome intrusive thoughts
🤖AI Coach's Note · reframe it in one line⌄

A phrase that lands well with juniors: "Pressure is a privilege — it means the moment matters and you're the one they trust in it." Say it in the car, put it on a locker sticker. Repeated calm framing teaches the brain that pressure = opportunity, not threat, long before game day.

Chapter 04

Effects of High-Pressure Situations

When the pressure hits, the fight-or-flight mechanism is triggered — leading to symptoms that affect gameplay. How many of these can your athlete recognise?

Tap each one you've felt — it saves as you go.

Increased heart rate & blood pressure — feeling nervous and overwhelmed
Impaired motor function (that "uncoordinated" feeling)
Increased muscle tension — reduced flexibility & movement
Impaired decision making
Unable to understand simple instructions from coach or teammates
Feeling like everyone is "against" you and you can't do anything right
🤖AI Coach's Note · the fastest reset that exists⌄

When the body flips into fight-or-flight, the quickest switch back is the breath. Teach box breathing: in for 4, hold 4, out for 4, hold 4 — three rounds. It's discreet enough to do at the free-throw line or on the bench, and it physically lowers heart rate so the "uncoordinated" feeling fades. Pair it with a long exhale (out longer than in) for an even faster calm.

Chapter 05

The Ultimate Feeling of a Strong Mindset

Calm under fire. Focused when it counts. Free to play.

A strong mindset feels like time slowing down — you see the play, you trust your training, and the noise (the crowd, the score, the last mistake) fades into the background. This is the state every tool in this blueprint is building toward.

Make it real: screenshot this page and set it as your athlete's phone wallpaper for the season — a daily reminder of the feeling they're training for.

Chapter 06

Building a Pre-Game Mindset Routine

Three easy pre-game tools before every game to elevate your mental strength. Don't skip these — they set you up for the next day.

1

Visualise the night before

Imagine the role you'll play, who your opposition will be, the moves you'll make (successfully) — and celebrating the win with your team afterwards.

2

Focus on two skills you'll do well

Ease the pressure by leaning into what you know you do well and can contribute. Pick two (max) and commit to doing them all game — despite anything that goes "wrong".

3

Reverse your bad habits under pressure

For every bad habit, choose a direct opposite action to trigger the moment you feel yourself slipping. E.g. replace eye-rolling with giving a teammate five, or speaking encouraging words.

Tap to build tonight's routine:

I visualised tomorrow's game last night
I chose my two go-to skills for this game
I have a reverse-action ready for my main bad habit
🤖AI Coach's Note · make the routine stick⌄

Habits form best when they're anchored to something that already happens. Tie visualisation to lights-out, and the "two skills" pick to putting shoes in the bag. Writing the two skills on a wristband or inside a shoe gives an in-game cue exactly when adrenaline makes them hard to recall.

Chapter 07

Confidence Through Challenge

How you react to tough calls, coaches' decisions and in-game adversity dictates how you play and the kind of teammate you are. Use these behaviour triggers to take control of your emotions.

Disagree with a ref callImmediately move on. Commit to creating positive impact within 30 seconds — be specific: get the next rebound, get a stop on defence.
Turnover (yours or a teammate's)Speak encouragement — internally for you, or directly to your teammate.
Critical mistake at a crucial timeHave an affirmation ready: "On to the next", "Next stop is mine", "There's X minutes left to create impact." Ask your coach to hold you accountable to the positive action.
Momentum swingsRemind yourself it's part of the game — the next swing is yours. Increase your intensity to make it happen sooner.
Self-doubtYou were put on that team for a reason. Simplify: focus on one or two skills you know you can do well.
Missing shotsCreate immediate impact elsewhere — defensive pressure, boxing out, assists. Ask your coach whether to keep shooting or look for the next pass.
Trash talkEasy one — ignore it. Nothing annoys a trash-talker more than an opponent who stays locked into their own game.
Social media pressureIf it's affecting your game, remove it. Your number-one priority is your performance and contribution to the team.
Extended time on the benchStay mentally in the game: study your likely match-ups, read what's working in offence, support teammates with high energy. Never sit away from the team, go quiet or sulk.
🤖AI Coach's Note · turn triggers into a wallet card⌄

Nine triggers is a lot to recall mid-game. Have your athlete pick their top three (the situations that actually derail them) and write just those on a small card for the kit bag. Fewer, rehearsed responses beat a long list they can't reach for under adrenaline.

Chapter 08

Communication Tools for Parents

A baseline of communication to support your junior athlete's mental fortitude — as a player and a teammate.

On the way to the game
1

"What do you do well?"

2

"What do you need to pay attention to?"

3

"What are two skills you can do well to create impact?"

Be led by your athlete on timing — an opportunity will present itself. With consistency, these conversations become easier and "normal".

On the way home / during the week
  • "What are you most proud of?" (they must have at least one)
  • "What's something you need to work on?"
  • "How does that translate to training this week — how can I help?"

Revisit these as checkpoints for whether actions match what was discussed. Teenage emotions are unpredictable, but keeping them connected to their commitment and goals makes it easier.

🤖AI Coach's Note · the one rule that keeps them talking⌄

Lead with pride before critique, always. Ask "what are you most proud of?" first, then "one thing to work on." And when they've had a rough game, resist coaching in the car — a simple "I loved watching you play" keeps the door open so the real conversation can happen later, on their terms.

Chapter 09

How to Make Permanent Changes

The steps so far help you change your mindset in the moment. But how do you build that into a permanent mindset that happens automatically?

Some say 21 days, some say 60. My advice: keep doing it until you realise it's automatic — however long that takes, because it's different for everyone. It might take two games, or half a season of small improvements.

Signs you're building a stronger mindset
  • Your reaction time reduces — to a play, a ref's call, a decision
  • You use the triggers more than you don't throughout the game
  • Your mind is focused on the team, not personal outcomes or mistakes
  • Coaches and teammates start to notice a positive change
  • You can move quickly on to the next play

At this point, actively practise positive self-talk based on the results you've noticed. A few key sentences before the game will help frame your mindset.

Chapter 10 · Workshop

Self-Assessment Workshop

Time to get specific. Ask your coach, mentor or parents for help here. You might not have three — but don't list more than three. That's too many to change at once. (Everything you type saves on this device.)

Three negative behaviour / thought patterns in your game — ranked most-impactful first.

Your triggers. Using Chapters 6 & 7, write a trigger you'll use to overcome each behaviour. If nothing fits, create your own — an action or thought that moves you on to the next play quickly.

Post-game assessment. What situation arose, which trigger you used, and how it impacted your game. Be honest — was the outcome positive or negative, and what could be different next time? Don't skip this step.

🤖AI Coach's Note · a rhythm for the reflection⌄

Reflection sticks when it's short and regular. Try three lines after every game: one win, one lesson, one focus for next time. A rolling log like this turns a season into visible progress — and makes the parent conversations in Chapter 8 effortless, because the athlete already knows their own answers.
